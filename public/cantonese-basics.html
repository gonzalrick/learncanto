Cantonese has six tones — pitch alone changes what a word means. Here are six everyday words you'll actually use, one sitting on each tone. Trace the contour, then say it out loud and feel where your voice goes.

Reading jyutping

  • The number after each syllable is the tone (1–6), not a count. nei5 hou2 = "hello".
  • oe / eo are rounded vowels with no English match — like the vowel in French "peu".
  • Initial ng- is a real sound (the end of "sing"). ng5 = the number five.
  • Many speakers swap initial n- and l- — nei5 often sounds like lei5. Both are fine.
  • Final -p -t -k are "stopped" — you shape the sound but barely release it.

Sounding natural fast

  • Learn 唔該 m4 goi1 first — it covers "please", "thank you", and "excuse me".
  • Don't chase perfect tones early. Context carries you; effort earns goodwill.
  • Say everything out loud. Tones live in muscle memory, not on the page.
  • Shadow real speakers — Hong Kong dramas, Cantopop, YouTube — and copy the rhythm.
